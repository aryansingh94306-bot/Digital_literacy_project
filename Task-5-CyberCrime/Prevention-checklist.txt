Stay Safe Online: A Checklist for College Students
1. Secure Your Credentials
Use unique, complex passwords for your university portal, Gmail, and social media. Avoid using your name, DOB, or "123456." Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on all primary accounts.

2. Verify UPI 'Request Money' Scams
Remember that entering your UPI PIN is only for sending money, never for receiving it. If someone sends you a "Request" to pay for a prize or a second-hand item, decline it immediately.

3. Official App Sources Only
Download banking, UPI (PhonePe, GPay), and academic apps only from the official Google Play Store or Apple App Store. Avoid "Modded" or "Premium" APKs, as they often contain keyloggers.

4. Sanitize Your Digital Footprint
Do not post photos of your Aadhaar card, Hall Ticket, or Internship Offer Letters on Instagram or LinkedIn. Blur out sensitive details like your Roll Number and Address.

5. Shield Your Banking SMS
Never share the 6-digit OTP or your ATM PIN with anyone, even if they claim to be calling from your bank's head office or a "technical support" team.

6. Public Wi-Fi Precautions
Avoid logging into your bank account or university portal while connected to free campus or cafe Wi-Fi. If necessary, use a trusted VPN to encrypt your connection.

7. Scrutinize Shortened Links
Be wary of Bitly or TinyURL links sent via WhatsApp groups promising "Free Internships" or "Laptops for Students." Use a link expander to check the destination before clicking.

8. Secure Your Hardware
Set a strong BIOS password and screen lock on your laptop. Since you are likely carrying your device between the hostel and campus, this prevents unauthorized physical access to your local files and saved browser passwords.

Reflection for Project Report (125 Words)
During the research for this project, the most surprising discovery was the sheer psychological sophistication behind "Social Engineering." I previously believed cybercrime was purely technical, but seeing how attackers leverage the urgency of university deadlines or the excitement of internship offers to bypass common sense was eye-opening. It is startling how easily a cloned login page can deceive even a tech-savvy student.

As a result, the one habit I will personally change is my reliance on "Auto-save Passwords" in browsers for sensitive accounts. Moving forward, I will transition to a dedicated, encrypted password manager and ensure that my university credentials have a unique password distinct from my social media. This layer of "compartmentalization" is a simple but vital step in preventing a single breach from cascading into total identity theft.
